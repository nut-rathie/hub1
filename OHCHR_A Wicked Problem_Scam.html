1. Why This Is a “Wicked Problem”

systems behavioural
  • Cyber scam operations are embedded in complex socio-economic, political, and digital ecosystems.
  • Traditional law enforcement responses have not disrupted recruitment dynamics.
  • Reframed through behavioural science (why people decide to migrate) + systems analysis (how structures shape and constrain decisions).
  • Prevention requires understanding decision-making under vulnerability, not just criminal networks.
What this changes
  • “More raids” alone won’t solve recruitment; the system adapts and replenishes labour.
  • Prevention needs levers across behaviour, platforms, and governance structures.

2. Behavioural Science Lens (COM-B Model)

behavioural

COM-B explains recruitment as a behaviour produced by Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation.

View COM-B breakdown
Capability
  • Basic digital literacy enabled participation in “online work”.
  • Legitimacy cues: interviews, HR departments, tests.
  • Low detection capability in complex digital recruitment settings.
Motivation
  • Financial pressure (debts, unemployment, post-COVID precarity).
  • Promised salary as dominant persuasion (68% critical).
  • Emotions: escape stagnation, family duty, urgency pressure.
  • Trust lowered risk perception.
Opportunity
  • Informal recruitment systems + weak labour regulation + low platform oversight.
  • ~74% recruited via trusted individuals/referrals.
  • Platforms amplify credibility and reach.
Key insight: decisions looked rational within constrained contexts (economic pressure + information asymmetry).

3. The Role of Trust in Recruitment

behavioural key lever prevention
  • Trust is the central behavioural lever.
  • Recruiters are often friends, family, co-workers, community members; some are victims themselves.
  • Group migration increases perceived safety.
  • Professionalized recruitment processes boost perceived legitimacy.
Implication for prevention
  • Interventions must target social proof and trust networks, not just generic awareness campaigns.

4. Technology as an Enabler of Trafficking

technology

Digital tech is structural to the scam business model, not peripheral.

A. Digital infrastructure
  • Large-scale ICT infrastructure across compounds.
  • AI scripts, deepfakes, multilingual content, automated targeting, crypto laundering systems.
B. AI in recruitment
  • Scrape social media for financial vulnerability signals.
  • Identify employment distress; remove language barriers; scale deception.
Why AI matters
  • Increases precision of targeting, speed of recruitment, and cross-border reach.

5. Social Media as Recruitment Architecture

technology systems
Primary channels
  • Facebook (most common), TikTok, Instagram, YouTube
  • Telegram, WhatsApp, Signal, LINE (encrypted migration)
Dynamics
  • Job ads in “legit” employment groups.
  • Rapid shift to encrypted messaging.
  • Fake HR processes simulate formal employment.
Moderation gaps
  • Counter-trafficking accounts sometimes removed.
  • Recruiters persist via algorithmic blind spots.
  • Limited local-language moderation capacity.
Technology insight: digital governance gaps create asymmetric advantage for traffickers.

6. System-Level Enablers

systems
  • Migration governance opacity
  • Weak labour inspection systems
  • Corruption and official collusion
  • Impunity
  • Weak digital regulatory regimes
  • Limited protection post-release
Without structural reform, behavioural interventions alone will fail.

7. Prevention Through Behavioural + Systems Integration

behavioural systems technology prevention
Behavioural interventions
  • Design counter-messaging based on real decision drivers.
  • Address trust-based pathways.
  • Disrupt urgency manipulation tactics.
  • Improve early detection of digital recruitment patterns.
Systems interventions
  • Regulate digital recruitment platforms.
  • Enforce human rights due diligence for tech companies.
  • Strengthen labour migration governance.
  • Implement non-punishment principles for victims forced into criminality.
  • Target corruption networks.

8. Core Strategic Insight

key insight prevention

The persistence of recruitment is not due to ignorance.

  • Driven by rational decisions under constraint
  • Amplified by digital technology
  • Embedded in fragile governance systems
